# Dementia SOP Reference

Use this reference when auditing dementia, MCI, cognitive decline, Alzheimer's disease, or neurodegenerative disease cohort designs.

## Core Concepts

- Dementia-free baseline must be defined using explicit evidence: clinical diagnosis, CDR, cognitive status label, diagnostic codes, or a justified combination.
- MCI and dementia should not be collapsed unless the research question explicitly supports doing so.
- Cognitive decline outcomes should define the instrument, direction, minimum meaningful change, and follow-up window.
- Incident dementia requires evidence that dementia was absent before or at baseline and first observed after follow-up begins.
- Progression from normal cognition to MCI/dementia differs from progression from MCI to dementia; do not mix without a stated estimand.
- APOE is usually time-invariant genetically, but its measurement date still matters for data availability and selection bias.
- Death and loss to follow-up can be informative and may need censoring, competing risk, or sensitivity analysis.

## Common Dementia Cohort Questions

- Is baseline the first visit, first eligible visit, first treatment date, or another anchor?
- Does baseline cognition come before treatment assignment?
- Is dementia exclusion based on diagnosis, CDR, cognitive status, medication, or a combined rule?
- Are cognitive scores comparable across visits, centers, languages, or instruments?
- Is "at least two visits" a design requirement, an outcome ascertainment requirement, or a source of selection bias?
- Are outcomes counted only after a clinically plausible latency period?
- Are education, age, sex, APOE, and baseline cognition measured before time zero?

## Red Flags

- Requiring future follow-up before defining baseline eligibility without discussing selection bias.
- Using post-baseline diagnosis, cognition, medication, or biomarker values as baseline covariates.
- Treating APOE availability as harmless when APOE testing is missing non-randomly.
- Defining treatment after baseline while using baseline covariates measured after treatment start.
- Ignoring death or dropout in cognitive decline studies.
- Reporting attrition without explaining which exclusion caused each count change.
